Electromagnetics

Saltwater Fresnel Zones.

Operating sub-GHz LoRa over the ocean introduces brutal physical constraints. Unlike a terrestrial deployment where signals pass through air and trees, seawater acts as a near-perfect reflective ground plane that aggressively induces multipath fading and signal cancellation.

Constraint

Earth Curvature

At an antenna height of just 1 meter (typical for a drifting buoy), the absolute geometric horizon is roughly 3.5 kilometers. Without atmospheric ducting, 900MHz RF physically cannot penetrate the earth's curvature past this point.

Constraint

Fresnel Blockage

The Fresnel Zone is the elliptical region between the TX and RX antennas. Over the ocean, massive 3-meter swells dynamically block the lower half of the first Fresnel zone, causing intermittent signal blackout.

Solution

Boosted Sensitivity

To combat multipath fading, OMEGA forces the SX126x transceivers into RX Boosted Gain Mode, achieving a receiver sensitivity of -137 dBm (+3dB improvement) at the cost of 2mA extra current draw.

Thermodynamics

The 1-Watt Thermal Envelope.

To punch through ocean swells and achieve true over-the-horizon transmission, OMEGA employs custom High Power Amplifiers (HPA) and the T-Beam 1W to push a blistering +30dBm (1 Watt) of RF output power. However, generating 1 Watt of RF inside a hermetically sealed IP67 waterproof enclosure creates a thermal crisis.

Silicon Constraints: The SX1262 chip itself is physically incapable of driving +30dBm; attempting to do so will permanently destroy the silicon. OMEGA safely caps the internal SX1262 PA at +22dBm, routing the signal into the external HPA.

TX-Burst Fan Control: To survive the thermal load, the ESP32-S3 physically spins up an external cooling fan on maximum GPIO drive just seconds before a transmission burst, and spins it down immediately after the packet clears the antenna. This prevents thermal throttling while strictly preserving the power budget during deep sleep.
