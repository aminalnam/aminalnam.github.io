OMEGA Architecture

The Hardware Fleet

OMEGA has transitioned from a single Arduino survey device into a synchronized, distributed fleet of autonomous rovers and multi-bearer relay nodes.

Physical Topology

The OMEGA physical layer relies on separating the network overlay from the physical radio bearer. By utilizing diverse radio hardware, the network pushes data from deep water to the shoreline gateway seamlessly.

The ASV Mobile Gateway

The flagship node of the fleet is the Autonomous Surface Vehicle (ASV). This is not just a sensor platform; it acts as an intelligent edge-compute gateway.

Pixhawk & Jetson Orin

The ASV is powered by a Pixhawk Jetson Baseboard Bundle paired with a Jetson Orin NX. While the Pixhawk handles low-level navigational autonomy and waypoint execution, the Jetson performs sensor fusion, live bathymetric calculations, and payload compression.

Mixed-Bearer Hardware

To ensure resilient communication in challenging marine environments, the ASV is heavily equipped with multiple radios:

  • LilyGO T-HaLow: Used for high-bandwidth IP backhaul when in range of the shore.
  • LilyGO T-Beam Supreme: Provides GNSS tracking and sub-GHz LoRa transmission.
  • LTE Modems: For fallback cloud connectivity when cellular networks are reachable.

Relay Chains & Sub-Surface Links

Water completely absorbs standard 2.4GHz Wi-Fi within inches, and sea-level environments are notoriously hostile to line-of-sight transmissions. OMEGA overcomes this through dynamic relay chains and specialized sub-surface protocols.

Disposable LoRa Relays

As the ASV moves further from the shoreline gateway, the direct RF link margin degrades due to wave scatter and Earth curvature. To preserve the control chain, the ASV can dynamically deploy cheap, floating LoRa relay nodes. The mission planner calculates optimal drop spacing based on real-time RSSI triggers, creating a breadcrumb path like Shore → Relay-01 → Relay-02 → ASV.

Acoustic vs. Optical Modems

For communication with fully submerged sensors, RF is abandoned entirely. OMEGA splits the underwater domain into two paradigms:

  • Acoustic Links: Provide long-range, low-bandwidth communication with high latency (seconds). Used for critical commands, health checks, and sparse telemetry.
  • Optical Links (Blue/Green): Provide short-range, high-bandwidth uploads but require line-of-sight and favorable water turbidity. Used for burst-dumping logs when a submersible docks with an ASV.

Edge Validation Strategy

Power consumption is the absolute limiting factor for autonomous marine nodes. By shifting data validation to the edge, the fleet drastically reduces radio transmission time.

Why We Validate Before Transmitting

A raw sonar transducer might ping at 10Hz, generating massive amounts of data. However, if the ASV is pitching at 20 degrees over a wave, or if the GPS fix has temporarily lost RTK lock, those depth soundings are mathematically useless for bathymetry.

Instead of transmitting everything over the constrained LoRa mesh, the Jetson Orin evaluates the IMU orientation and GPS variance in real-time. It only packages and transmits data packets that meet strict quality thresholds, effectively acting as an active data filter. This reduces mesh network congestion by over 85%, leaving bandwidth open for critical mission commands.
